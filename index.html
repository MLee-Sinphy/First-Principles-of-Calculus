About the Project

First Principles of Calculus is an open educational project whose primary objective is to reconstruct differential and integral calculus from its logical foundations. Rather than presenting isolated computational techniques, the project seeks to develop every concept from carefully stated assumptions, precise definitions, and rigorous mathematical arguments.

The exposition follows a modern perspective inspired by mathematical analysis. Historical context, intuitive motivation, formal definitions, theorems, proofs, examples, and exercises are presented as complementary aspects of a coherent mathematical theory, allowing intuition and rigor to develop together.

Although initially focused on calculus, this project is intended to become part of a broader collection of mathematical notes covering several areas of mathematics and theoretical physics, all sharing the same commitment to conceptual clarity and mathematical precision.

Why Study Calculus?

Calculus represents one of the greatest intellectual achievements in the history of mathematics. Independently developed during the seventeenth century by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, it provided the mathematical language necessary to describe continuous change, motion, geometry, accumulation, and countless natural phenomena.

Today, calculus constitutes one of the fundamental pillars of modern science. Virtually every branch of mathematics, physics, engineering, computer science, economics, quantitative finance, and machine learning relies upon concepts that originate directly from differential and integral calculus.

Learning calculus rigorously does far more than provide tools for solving equations. It develops mathematical maturity, logical reasoning, abstraction, and a deeper appreciation for the structure upon which modern mathematical analysis is built.

Limits

Limits form the conceptual foundation of differential and integral calculus. They provide the precise mathematical language required to describe approximation, continuity, infinitesimal variation, and the behavior of functions as their variables approach specific values.

Beyond their theoretical importance, limits appear naturally throughout mathematics, physics, engineering, economics, computer science, and virtually every discipline that studies continuous systems. They transform intuitive ideas of "getting arbitrarily close" into precise mathematical statements.

This chapter begins with geometric intuition and historical motivation before gradually developing the modern ε-δ definition, establishing the rigorous framework upon which continuity, derivatives, and integrals are built.

Derivatives

The derivative was developed to answer one of the most fundamental questions in science: how can we describe instantaneous change? Whether studying the velocity of a moving particle, the growth of a population, or the evolution of a physical system, the concept of derivative provides the appropriate mathematical language.

More than a computational tool, differentiation reveals the local structure of functions, allowing us to understand linear approximation, optimization, geometry, and the dynamics of continuous phenomena.

Beginning with the notion of average rate of change, this chapter develops the derivative from first principles, culminating in a rigorous treatment of differentiability and its fundamental properties.

Integrals

Many quantities encountered in mathematics and science arise through accumulation. Distance traveled, probability, mass, electric charge, energy, and area are all examples of quantities that cannot be understood by examining a single point in isolation.

Integration provides the mathematical framework through which infinitely many infinitesimal contributions combine into a single meaningful quantity. It is one of the deepest and most powerful ideas in all of mathematics.

Starting from partitions and Riemann sums, this chapter develops the integral rigorously before establishing the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, one of the central results connecting differentiation and integration.
