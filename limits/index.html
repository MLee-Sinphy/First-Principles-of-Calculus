Chapter I

The theory of limits provides the formal language through which mathematics describes approximation, convergence, continuity, instantaneous variation, and accumulation. Differential and integral calculus cannot be constructed rigorously without it.

The purpose of this chapter is not merely to teach techniques for evaluating limits. Its principal objective is to explain what a limit is, why the concept is necessary, how its modern definition encodes approximation through quantifiers, and how rigorous proofs are built directly from that definition.

1. Motivation

Mathematics frequently studies the behavior of a quantity near a point rather than exclusively at that point. A function may be undefined at \(x=a\), yet its values may exhibit a completely determinate pattern whenever \(x\) approaches \(a\). Conversely, a function may be defined at \(a\), while its nearby behavior bears no relation to the assigned value \(f(a)\).

Consider the function

\[ f(x) = \frac{x^2-1}{x-1}, \qquad x\neq 1. \]

Algebraically,

\[ x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1), \]

and therefore

\[ f(x)=x+1, \qquad x\neq 1. \]

Although \(f(1)\) is not defined, the values of \(f(x)\) become arbitrarily close to \(2\) when \(x\) becomes sufficiently close to \(1\). The concept of limit isolates precisely this local behavior:

\[ \lim_{x\to 1} \frac{x^2-1}{x-1} =2. \]

Conceptual distinction

The expression \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L\) concerns the values of \(f(x)\) for \(x\) near \(a\). It does not, by itself, assert that \(f(a)\) exists, nor that \(f(a)=L\).

Limits therefore allow us to discuss instantaneous velocity, tangent lines, continuity, infinite series, derivatives, integrals, differential equations, numerical approximation, probability distributions, and countless other structures arising throughout mathematics, physics, engineering, and computational science.

2. Historical Context

The methods that eventually became differential and integral calculus were developed during the seventeenth century, especially through the work of Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz. Early calculus successfully solved profound problems involving motion, tangency, area, and accumulation, but its foundational language relied heavily on geometric intuition and infinitesimal quantities whose logical status was not yet completely clarified.

During the nineteenth century, mathematicians progressively reconstructed analysis on an arithmetic and logical basis. Bernard Bolzano's 1817 work presented an unusually advanced purely analytic treatment of continuity and the intermediate value property [1]. Augustin-Louis Cauchy's Cours d'Analyse, published in 1821, systematically placed limits and convergence near the center of analysis [2].

The explicitly quantified \(\varepsilon\)-\(\delta\) formulation familiar today emerged gradually and reached a mature form in the work and lectures of Karl Weierstrass during the nineteenth century [3]. This development replaced vague expressions such as “infinitely close” with exact statements concerning inequalities and quantifiers.

Historical note

The history of rigor in analysis was not a simple replacement of error by correctness. Earlier methods were often computationally powerful and conceptually subtle. The nineteenth-century development supplied a framework in which those insights could be stated and proved with greater logical precision.

3. Logical and Mathematical Preliminaries

A rigorous definition of limit depends upon several ideas: distance on the real line, punctured neighborhoods, accumulation points, implication, and nested quantifiers. We introduce these objects before stating the definition.

Definition 3.1 — Distance on \(\mathbb{R}\)

For \(x,a\in\mathbb{R}\), the distance between \(x\) and \(a\) is

\[ d(x,a)\coloneqq |x-a|. \]

Definition 3.2 — Open and punctured neighborhoods

Let \(a\in\mathbb{R}\) and \(\delta>0\). The open \(\delta\)-neighborhood of \(a\) is

\[ B_\delta(a) \coloneqq \left\{ x\in\mathbb{R} : |x-a|<\delta \right\}. \]

The corresponding punctured neighborhood is

\[ B_\delta^{\times}(a) \coloneqq \left\{ x\in\mathbb{R} : 0<|x-a|<\delta \right\}. \]

The condition \(0<|x-a|\) excludes the point \(a\) itself.

Definition 3.3 — Accumulation point

Let \(D\subseteq\mathbb{R}\). A point \(a\in\mathbb{R}\) is an accumulation point of \(D\) when

\[ \forall \delta>0, \qquad B_\delta^{\times}(a)\cap D \neq \varnothing. \]

Equivalently, every punctured neighborhood of \(a\) contains at least one point of \(D\).

Why accumulation points matter

To discuss the behavior of \(f(x)\) as \(x\to a\), the domain of \(f\) must contain points arbitrarily close to \(a\). The point \(a\) itself need not belong to the domain.

The quantifier pattern

The formal limit definition has the logical form

\[ \forall\varepsilon>0, \quad \exists\delta>0, \quad \forall x\in D, \quad P(\varepsilon,\delta,x). \]

The order is essential. The value of \(\delta\) may depend on \(\varepsilon\), but it must not depend on the particular point \(x\) subsequently chosen.

4. The Informal Concept of Limit

Informally, the statement \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L\) means that values of \(f(x)\) can be forced arbitrarily close to \(L\) by requiring \(x\) to be sufficiently close to \(a\), without requiring \(x=a\).

The phrases “arbitrarily close” and “sufficiently close” encode two independent tolerances:

\(\varepsilon\)
The permitted error in the output: \(|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon\).
\(\delta\)
The required proximity in the input: \(0<|x-a|<\delta\).

The definition asserts that every positive output tolerance can be achieved by imposing a sufficiently restrictive positive input tolerance.

\[ 0<|x-a|<\delta \quad\Longrightarrow\quad |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon. \]

What the definition does not say

It does not say that \(x\) eventually becomes equal to \(a\), that \(f(x)\) becomes equal to \(L\), or that either quantity is physically moving. The definition is a quantified statement about inequalities.

5. The Formal Definition

Definition 5.1 — Limit of a real-valued function

Let \(D\subseteq\mathbb{R}\), let \(f:D\to\mathbb{R}\), and let \(a\) be an accumulation point of \(D\). We say that \(f(x)\) tends to \(L\in\mathbb{R}\) as \(x\) tends to \(a\), and write

\[ \boxed{ \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L }, \]

when

\[ \boxed{ \forall\varepsilon>0\ \exists\delta>0\ \forall x\in D: \quad 0<|x-a|<\delta \Longrightarrow |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon }. \]

Reading the definition

The definition can be read as a challenge-and-response structure:

  1. An arbitrary tolerance \(\varepsilon>0\) is specified.
  2. One must produce a corresponding \(\delta>0\).
  3. The chosen \(\delta\) must work for every \(x\in D\) satisfying \(0<|x-a|<\delta\).
  4. For every such \(x\), one must prove \(|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon\).

Dependence of \(\delta\)

In general, \(\delta\) may depend on \(\varepsilon\), on the function \(f\), and on the point \(a\). A proof often constructs an explicit rule \(\delta=\delta(\varepsilon)\), though the definition requires only existence.

Theorem 5.2 — Uniqueness of limits

Let \(a\) be an accumulation point of \(D\subseteq\mathbb{R}\), and let \(f:D\to\mathbb{R}\). If

\[ \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L \qquad\text{and}\qquad \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=M, \]

then \(L=M\).

Proof

Assume, toward a contradiction, that \(L\neq M\). Define

\[ \varepsilon \coloneqq \frac{|L-M|}{3} >0. \]

Since \(f(x)\to L\), there exists \(\delta_L>0\) such that

\[ 0<|x-a|<\delta_L \Longrightarrow |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon. \]

Since \(f(x)\to M\), there exists \(\delta_M>0\) such that

\[ 0<|x-a|<\delta_M \Longrightarrow |f(x)-M|<\varepsilon. \]

Let

\[ \delta \coloneqq \min\{\delta_L,\delta_M\}. \]

Because \(a\) is an accumulation point of \(D\), there exists \(x\in D\) such that \(0<|x-a|<\delta\). By the triangle inequality,

\[ \begin{aligned} |L-M| &= |L-f(x)+f(x)-M| \\ &\leq |L-f(x)|+|f(x)-M| \\ &< \varepsilon+\varepsilon \\ &= \frac{2}{3}|L-M|. \end{aligned} \]

This is impossible. Therefore \(L=M\). \(\square\)

6. First Examples

Example 6.1 — A linear function

Prove directly from the definition that

\[ \lim_{x\to 3}(2x+1)=7. \]

We seek to control

\[ \begin{aligned} |f(x)-7| &= |2x+1-7| \\ &= |2x-6| \\ &= 2|x-3|. \end{aligned} \]

Given \(\varepsilon>0\), choose

\[ \delta \coloneqq \frac{\varepsilon}{2}. \]

If \(0<|x-3|<\delta\), then

\[ |f(x)-7| = 2|x-3| < 2\delta = \varepsilon. \]

Hence,

\[ \lim_{x\to 3}(2x+1)=7. \qquad \square \]

Example 6.2 — A quadratic function

Prove that

\[ \lim_{x\to 2}x^2=4. \]

The relevant difference factors as

\[ |x^2-4| = |x-2||x+2|. \]

The factor \(|x-2|\) is controlled directly by \(\delta\), but \(|x+2|\) must also be bounded. We first impose

\[ |x-2|<1. \]

Then \(1 \[ |x+2|<5. \]

Given \(\varepsilon>0\), choose

\[ \delta \coloneqq \min \left\{ 1, \frac{\varepsilon}{5} \right\}. \]

If \(0<|x-2|<\delta\), then

\[ \begin{aligned} |x^2-4| &= |x-2||x+2| \\ &< 5|x-2| \\ &< 5\delta \\ &\leq \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \]

Therefore,

\[ \lim_{x\to 2}x^2=4. \qquad \square \]

Example 6.3 — A removable singularity

Consider

\[ f(x) = \frac{x^2-1}{x-1}, \qquad x\neq 1. \]

For \(x\neq1\),

\[ f(x)=x+1. \]

Thus,

\[ \begin{aligned} |f(x)-2| &= |x+1-2| \\ &= |x-1|. \end{aligned} \]

Given \(\varepsilon>0\), choosing \(\delta=\varepsilon\) proves that

\[ \lim_{x\to1} \frac{x^2-1}{x-1} = 2. \]

Notice again that the function need not be defined at the limiting point.

Theorem 7.3 — Characterization by one-sided limits

Suppose that the relevant one-sided accumulation conditions hold. Then

\[ \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L \]

if and only if

\[ \lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)=L \qquad\text{and}\qquad \lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)=L. \]

Example 7.4 — Distinct one-sided limits

Let

\[ f(x) = \begin{cases} -1, & x<0,\\ 1, & x\geq0. \end{cases} \]

Then

\[ \lim_{x\to0^-}f(x)=-1, \qquad \lim_{x\to0^+}f(x)=1. \]

Since the one-sided limits are different,

\[ \lim_{x\to0}f(x) \]

does not exist.

8. Fundamental Limit Laws

Theorem 8.1 — Algebra of limits

Suppose

\[ \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L, \qquad \lim_{x\to a}g(x)=M, \]

and let \(\lambda\in\mathbb{R}\). Then:

  1. \[ \lim_{x\to a} \bigl(f(x)+g(x)\bigr) = L+M. \]
  2. \[ \lim_{x\to a} \lambda f(x) = \lambda L. \]
  3. \[ \lim_{x\to a} f(x)g(x) = LM. \]
  4. If \(M\neq0\), then \[ \lim_{x\to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M}, \] provided \(g(x)\neq0\) in a sufficiently small punctured neighborhood of \(a\).

Why these laws matter

The definition remains the logical foundation of the theory, but the limit laws allow complicated expressions to be reduced to simpler ones. Their proofs are not mere formalities: they show how error tolerances combine under algebraic operations.

10. Exercises

The exercises are ordered from conceptual interpretation to formal proof.

  1. Level I — Conceptual

    Explain why the value \(f(a)\) does not appear in the formal definition of \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\).

  2. Level II — Logical structure

    Negate the statement

    \[ \forall\varepsilon>0\ \exists\delta>0\ \forall x\in D: \quad 0<|x-a|<\delta \Longrightarrow |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon. \]

    Express the result both symbolically and in words.

  3. Level III — Direct proof

    Prove from the definition that

    \[ \lim_{x\to -2}(3x-4)=-10. \]

  4. Level IV — Bounding argument

    Prove from the definition that

    \[ \lim_{x\to3}x^2=9. \]

    Your proof must explicitly justify a bound for \(|x+3|\).

  5. Level V — Challenge

    Let

    \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} x\sin(1/x), & x\neq0,\\ 0, & x=0. \end{cases} \]

    Prove directly from the \(\varepsilon\)-\(\delta\) definition that

    \[ \lim_{x\to0}f(x)=0. \]

    Identify precisely which inequality controls the oscillatory factor.
